Briefing 01 // Core Concepts

The Radio Spectrum

The radio spectrum is a range of invisible energy waves that travel through the air at the speed of light. Every wireless device — from your radio to your phone — uses a slice of it.

// Key Concept
Frequency = how fast a wave wiggles
Imagine shaking a jump rope slowly — that's a low frequency. Shake it fast — that's a high frequency. Radio waves work the same way. We measure frequency in Hertz (Hz). 1 Hz means one full wave per second.
// Field Analogy
Like lanes on a highway
The spectrum is like a highway with thousands of lanes. Each "lane" is a different frequency. FM radio, cell phones, Wi-Fi, and satellites all use different lanes so their signals don't collide.
// AM Radio
530–1700 kHz
Long range, penetrates buildings. Used for talk radio and news.
// FM Radio
88–108 MHz
Better sound quality, shorter range. Music stations.
// Wi-Fi
2.4 / 5 GHz
Very high frequency, great for fast data. Home internet.
// Cell Phones
700 MHz – 3.5 GHz
Wide range for calls, texts, and mobile data.
Briefing 02 // Transmission Protocol

How Radios Work

A radio transmits information by encoding it onto a wave — like writing a message on a wave and sending it through the air at the speed of light.

01
Generate a carrier wave. A transmitter produces a radio wave at a specific frequency. This is the empty "carrier" that will hold your message.
02
Modulate the signal. Your voice or music is layered onto the carrier wave. AM changes the wave's height (amplitude). FM changes the wave's speed (frequency).
03
Transmit via antenna. The antenna broadcasts the wave outward in all directions at 300,000,000 meters per second — the speed of light.
04
Receive and decode. A receiving antenna captures the wave. Your radio tunes to the correct frequency and strips the carrier away, leaving only the original audio.
// AM vs FM
Two types of modulation
AM (Amplitude Modulation) — Changes the wave's height to carry information. Travels farther but picks up interference and static.

FM (Frequency Modulation) — Changes the wave's wiggle speed. Much clearer audio, but shorter range through hills and buildings.

Briefing 04 // Signal Mathematics

Wave Math

Every radio wave has a frequency and a wavelength. They're connected by a fundamental physics formula you can use right now.

λ = c ÷ f
Wavelength = Speed of Light ÷ Frequency · c = 300,000,000 m/s
As frequency goes up, wavelength goes down — they are inversely proportional. A Wi-Fi signal at 2.4 GHz has a wavelength of about 12 cm. An AM radio wave at 1 MHz is 300 meters long.
Frequency 100 MHz
// Field Application
Antenna size depends on wavelength
Antennas are designed to be roughly 1/4 the wavelength they receive. That's why AM towers are massive structures and your Wi-Fi router has a tiny stub antenna.

Briefing 06 // Historical Archive

History of Radio

Radio changed the world — from the first invisible waves predicted on paper to the wireless network powering every device you own.

// 1864
Maxwell Predicts Radio Waves
Scottish physicist James Clerk Maxwell mathematically predicted that invisible electromagnetic waves could travel through space — before anyone had built a machine to make them.
// 1888
Hertz Proves It
German physicist Heinrich Hertz built a device that actually produced and detected radio waves in a lab. The unit of frequency — the Hertz — is named in his honor.
// 1895–1901
Marconi: First Transatlantic Signal
Italian inventor Guglielmo Marconi sent the first long-distance radio signals. In 1901, he transmitted the letter "S" in Morse code from England to Canada — crossing the Atlantic Ocean. He won the Nobel Prize in 1909.
// 1906
First Voice Broadcast
Reginald Fessenden broadcast the first-ever human voice and music over radio on Christmas Eve. Sailors with radio receivers on ships were astonished to hear a human voice instead of Morse code.
// 1920s
Commercial Radio Begins
The first commercial radio stations launched across the US. By the late 1920s, millions of families had radio sets in their homes — it was the internet of its day.
// 1939–1945
Radio in World War II
Radio became critical military infrastructure. Armies used encrypted radio comms, radar was developed to detect enemy aircraft, and civilians relied on broadcasts for wartime news. The BBC broadcast coded messages to resistance fighters in France.
// 1933
FM Radio Invented
Edwin Armstrong invented FM radio, delivering far clearer audio than AM. Despite intense opposition from the AM broadcasting industry, FM eventually became the standard for music transmission.
// 1970s – Present
Wireless Everything
Radio technology evolved into cell phones, GPS, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and satellite communication. Today virtually every device you own uses radio waves to communicate — including the one you may be reading this on.
// Classified Intel
Your broadcasts are traveling through space right now
Radio signals from Earth's earliest TV broadcasts are still traveling outward through space at the speed of light. Signals from the 1940s have now passed over 80 light-years away.
